What is IoT?

In a nutshell, IoT is an abbreviation for the Internet of Things and it refers to the connectivity of electronic and other devices to the internet. These Things can then be monitored and controlled remotely depending on the use-case.
There are so many different areas of business and kinds of people who would benefit from IoT:
The General Population
These are users of products who will gain greater control over the monitoring of and use and management of the equipment they use.

An example of this is a connected home where many devices like lights, kettles, TV’s, automatic blinds, windows, door locks, etc. are connected to the Internet behind a secure firewall and can be monitored and controlled by an occupant of the home. Users will also benefit from accelerated production change cycles resulting from producers having better real-time insight regarding how products are used and cost-savings as a result of the introduction of pay-per-use costing models on certain products.

Producers
These are companies that produce products or equipment and could benefit from better insight as to their use or could devise better pricing models based on use.

To illustrate this, consider a manufacturer of electric drills. If the appropriate sensors are built into the drill, the manufacturer could get near-real-time information on the speed at which the drill is used, the pressure the drill is used under, the temperature of the components, etc. This would empower the producer (or their distributors) to more accurately recommend more suitable models to their clients. It would also provide valuable feedback to assist with the improvement of points-of-failure and would allow them to develop better pay-per-use pricing models.

Industry
These are companies that could benefit from better real-time insight as to their manufacturing plants or storage units or agricultural needs. There are a plethora of sensors and technologies that can be implemented into an IoT platform that will allow industry to better monitor, manage and control all areas of business. Industry can also benefit from cost-savings that result from predictive maintenance.

An example of this might be a motor vehicle manufacturing plant that needs to monitor the robots that do much of the welding and assembling of the vehicles they produce. Any downtime can result in massive losses. Every aspect of the manufacturing equipment can be monitored so that based on thresholds developed over time via machine learning, parts that are starting to give problems can be swapped out during planned outages before they break.
